Instagram Reels vs. YouTube Shorts: Where Should Your Budget Go?

We compared CPM, engagement and conversion data across both platforms for five client campaigns — here's what won.

EN

Enovexa Solutions — Marketing Team

May 19, 2026 · 5 min read

"Reels or Shorts?" is one of the most common questions we get from clients setting a social ad budget, and most agencies answer it with an opinion instead of a number. We decided to stop guessing and run an actual controlled test — same creative concept, same budget split, same landing page — across five live client campaigns over six weeks.

The results weren't as one-sided as either platform's sales team would have you believe. Each platform won on different metrics, for different client types, and the "cheaper" platform on paper wasn't always the cheaper platform once a sale actually closed. Here's exactly what we tracked and what it told us.

i
How we ran the test

Five clients — an ecommerce beauty brand, a SaaS tool, a local home-services business, an online course creator, and a DTC apparel brand — each ran the same offer as a native-length Reel and a native Shorts ad, with budget split 50/50, identical targeting, and the same landing page. We tracked CPM, engagement rate, conversion rate and cost per acquisition (CPA) for six weeks.

01The Numbers: CPM, Engagement & Conversion Side-By-Side

Averaged across all five campaigns, the two platforms behaved almost like mirror images of each other — Reels won on cost and attention, Shorts won on intent:

  • CPM (cost per 1,000 impressions) — Instagram Reels averaged $4.20, roughly 38% cheaper than YouTube Shorts at $6.80. Reels reach was consistently the cheaper way to buy raw attention.
  • Engagement rate (likes, comments, shares and saves per reach) — Reels averaged 5.8% versus 3.1% on Shorts, helped by Instagram's native share-to-story and save behavior.
  • Conversion rate (landing page visits to purchase or lead) — Shorts edged ahead at 2.9% versus 2.3% on Reels, particularly on higher-consideration offers.
  • CPA (cost per acquisition) — Despite the cheaper CPM, Reels produced a higher average CPA in two of the five campaigns, because cheap reach that doesn't convert is still an expensive customer.

02Where Each Platform Actually Won

Instagram Reels won for:

Impulse-driven ecommerce and DTC products, lower-funnel remarketing to warm audiences, and pure top-of- funnel awareness at scale. The beauty and apparel clients — both low-price, low-consideration purchases — saw their best blended ROAS running almost entirely on Reels.

YouTube Shorts won for:

Higher-ticket and higher-consideration offers — the SaaS tool and the online course both closed more efficiently through Shorts, likely because Shorts sits inside a platform people already use with intent to learn or research, and it skewed toward a slightly older, higher-income audience in our data.

"Cheaper reach doesn't always mean cheaper customers — the platform with the lowest CPM produced the highest CPA in two of five campaigns."

— Enovexa Marketing Team

03How We Actually Split Budget Now

We stopped treating this as an either/or decision. Based on the test, here's the default starting split we now recommend before optimizing further with live data:

Client type Recommended starting split
DTC / impulse ecommerce 70% Reels / 30% Shorts
SaaS / B2B lead gen 35% Reels / 65% Shorts
Local service business 55% Reels / 45% Shorts
Online courses / education 40% Reels / 60% Shorts

None of these are fixed rules — they're a starting point we adjust after two to three weeks of live spend on each new account, once CPA data is reliable enough to trust.

04Budget Allocation Checklist

Before you commit a full budget to either platform, run through this list — it's the exact process we use on every new client media plan:

  • Run the same creative concept, adapted to each platform's native length and captions, before comparing results
  • Split budget evenly at the start so early data isn't skewed by spend weighting
  • Track CPA and post-purchase LTV, not just CPM — cheap reach can still be an expensive customer
  • Give each test at least two to three weeks before drawing conclusions
  • Match platform to purchase intent: impulse buys lean Reels, considered purchases lean Shorts
  • Use the same landing page and offer across both platforms to isolate the variable that matters
  • Re-test quarterly — audience behavior and platform algorithms shift faster than most budgets do
  • Don't ignore creative fatigue — refresh assets before blaming the platform for declining performance
  • Segment results by product price point, not just by overall account average
  • Let live CPA data override the starting split within the first month of spend

🎯 Key Takeaway

  • Instagram Reels delivers cheaper reach and stronger engagement across nearly every campaign type
  • YouTube Shorts converts better on higher-ticket, higher-consideration offers
  • The platform with the lowest CPM isn't always the one with the lowest CPA — track both
  • Budget splits should be set by product type and revisited with live CPA data, not fixed permanently

The right answer isn't "Reels" or "Shorts" — it's whichever platform matches how your specific audience actually buys. If you haven't run a real side-by-side test on your own account yet, that's the first step before shifting any meaningful budget. We're happy to help you set one up.
